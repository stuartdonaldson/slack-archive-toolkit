# F3 Culture Context

_Last updated from the uploaded Slack digest generated 2026-06-30. Use this as background context for newsletters, onboarding notes, regional summaries, and LLM prompts. It is not an official F3 doctrine document; verify against official F3 sources when precision matters._

## Core cultural frame

F3 exists to develop and invigorate male community leadership. In the uploaded Slack digest, Columbia summarized the culture this way: to understand “the true DNA” of what happens “in the gloom,” PAX should understand F3’s origin story. His framing was that culture is not just something F3 talks about; it is central to why F3 exists and why PAX show up.

Use this as the default background:

- F3 is more than workouts. Fitness is the entry point, but the broader purpose is leadership, fellowship, and service.
- The “gloom” is where men show up together, do hard things, build trust, and learn to lead.
- Leadership is expected to spread. PAX are encouraged to move from attending to leading.
- F3 culture is intentionally decentralized. Men are “freed to lead” rather than waiting for permission from a formal hierarchy.
- The culture is practical and action-oriented: show up, Q, EH, serve, support other PAX, and give F3 away.

## Leadership posture

Columbia’s Cascades leadership note emphasized moving from building out to building up: growing the number of PAX and, more importantly, growing leaders. He framed F3 as a volunteer organization that should empower men who are ready to do more, join leadership, or lead an event or initiative.

When summarizing or generating content about F3 culture, use this leadership posture:

- Assume leadership is developed through participation, not credentials.
- Treat Q-ing, starting initiatives, and supporting AOs as visible leadership acts.
- Avoid framing leadership as limited to titled roles like Nantan, Weasel Shaker, SLT, or Site Q.
- Encourage men to step up without implying that every man needs the same role or level of commitment.
- Recognize both formal leadership and informal leadership: encouragement, consistency, service, and showing up for other PAX.

## “Freed to Lead”

“Freed to Lead” is a recurring cultural theme in the digest. Columbia pointed PAX to *Freed to Lead* as the F3 origin story and “blueprint” for understanding how F3 started and why F3 does what it does. He also tied “Freed to Lead” to practical regional leadership: PAX launching AOs, taking Qs, and owning initiatives.

Use this as shorthand:

- **Freed to Lead** means PAX are expected and trusted to act.
- Men do not need to wait for a perfect plan, a formal title, or permission to contribute.
- Healthy regions create room for new leaders rather than concentrating ownership in a few men.
- A good cultural signal is whether PAX can discover how to help, who to ask, and where to plug in.

## The 3 Fs

Use the 3 Fs as the basic organizing language:

1. **Fitness / 1st F** — workouts, rucks, CSAUPs, races, challenges, and physical acceleration.
2. **Fellowship / 2nd F** — coffeeteria, family events, 2.0 events, social gatherings, accountability, and friendship.
3. **Faith / 3rd F** — service, purpose, reflection, values, and community impact. Do not assume a single religious meaning; use the local context and source language.

## Common cultural terms

| Term | Working meaning |
|---|---|
| PAX | The men participating in F3. |
| HIM | High Impact Man; a man seeking to lead and serve in his relationships and community. |
| Gloom | The early-morning outdoor setting where many F3 workouts happen. |
| Q | The man leading a workout, discussion, event, or initiative. |
| VQ | A first Q. Often treated as a milestone to support. |
| Site Q | The person with local responsibility for an AO/site. |
| AO | Area of operations; a workout/site/channel/community location. |
| EH | Emotional headlock; inviting a man to come out to F3. |
| 2.0 | A PAX member’s child. |
| M | A PAX member’s spouse/significant other. |
| CSAUP | A challenging event, usually bigger than a normal workout. |
| Coffeeteria | Post-workout coffee/fellowship. |
| Downrange | Visiting or participating outside one’s home region/AO. |

## Culture cues from the digest

Use these recurring cues when interpreting posts:

- **Support the VQ.** When someone leads for the first time, PAX are encouraged to show up and support him.
- **Celebrate acceleration.** Posts often recognize PAX who Q frequently, step into Site Q roles, launch AOs, or take on initiatives.
- **Give F3 away.** Columbia described local media exposure as a way to make sure more men know there is a place for them in F3.
- **Leave it better.** Earth Day cleanup was framed as taking the “Community” part of the mission literally.
- **Iron sharpens iron.** Men are encouraged to recover from setbacks, swallow pride, and get back in the fight.
- **Remember the why.** Columbia encouraged PAX to update their F3 Nation profile fields such as who EH’d them, when they started, their origin story, and their why.
- **Leadership includes courage under hardship.** Ozone’s response to the F3 Nation podcast highlighted leadership shown through courage and strength in the face of terminal illness.

## Practical guidance for generated content

When generating F3-related summaries, newsletters, prompts, or onboarding material:

- Do not reduce F3 to exercise. Mention leadership, fellowship, and service when relevant.
- Do not over-explain insider language, but define terms when writing for newer PAX.
- Prefer F3 names/display names over real names.
- Treat leadership titles cautiously; titles in Slack profiles may be stale.
- Distinguish current role, former role, and informal leadership.
- Preserve Slack source links when summarizing posts.
- Avoid assuming an event happened unless later posts confirm the outcome.
- Highlight opportunities for PAX to act: Q, EH, serve, attend, donate, help organize, or update profile information.
- Avoid making the tone too corporate. F3 voice is practical, direct, encouraging, and often playful.
